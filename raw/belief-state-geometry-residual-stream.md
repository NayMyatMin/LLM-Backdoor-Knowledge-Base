# Transformers Represent Belief State Geometry in their Residual Stream

**Authors:** Adam S. Shai, Sarah E. Marzen, Lucas Teixeira, Alexander Gietelink Oldenziel, Paul M. Riechers
**Venue:** NeurIPS 2024
**URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.15943

## Abstract

This paper demonstrates that transformers learn to linearly represent belief states — probabilistic representations of the model's uncertainty about the underlying data-generating process — in their residual stream. Using Hidden Markov Models (HMMs) as controlled data-generating processes with known ground-truth belief states, the authors show that even when the predicted belief state geometry has highly nontrivial fractal structure, transformers learn to represent it linearly in activation space. The residual stream at each layer reflects the model's accumulated understanding, with updates refining the belief incrementally through the network. This provides a geometric and information-theoretic account of what transformers compute layer by layer.

## Key Contributions

1. **Linear belief state encoding**: Demonstrates that belief states are linearly recoverable from the residual stream using simple probes, even when the underlying geometry is fractal
2. **Layer-wise belief refinement**: Shows that belief representations improve progressively through layers, with each layer refining the model's probabilistic understanding
3. **Controlled experimental framework**: Uses HMMs with known ground-truth belief states, enabling precise quantification of how well transformers capture the true data-generating process
4. **Geometric analysis**: Characterizes the geometry of learned belief representations, showing they preserve the topological structure of the true belief simplex
5. **Theoretical connection**: Links transformer computation to optimal Bayesian inference over hidden states, providing a principled account of what residual stream updates accomplish

## Method

- Train transformers on sequences generated by Hidden Markov Models with known transition and emission matrices
- Extract residual stream activations at each layer for each token position
- Train linear probes to predict the ground-truth belief state (posterior distribution over HMM hidden states) from residual stream activations
- Measure probe accuracy layer by layer to characterize belief refinement dynamics
- Analyze the geometry of learned representations using dimensionality reduction (PCA, UMAP) and compare to the true belief state geometry
- Vary HMM complexity (number of hidden states, fractal structure of belief simplex) to test generality
- Compare against baselines including nonlinear probes and random projections

## Key Results

- Linear probes achieve near-perfect recovery of belief states from intermediate and later layers of the residual stream
- Belief state accuracy improves monotonically through layers, with the largest gains in early-to-middle layers
- The geometry of learned representations faithfully mirrors the true belief state geometry, including fractal structures
- Even for HMMs with 8+ hidden states and complex belief simplexes, linear encoding suffices
- Early layers encode coarse belief approximations; later layers encode fine-grained distinctions
- The residual stream updates (differences between consecutive layers) correspond to Bayesian belief updates
- Nonlinear probes offer minimal improvement over linear ones, confirming that the encoding is genuinely linear

## Significance

This paper provides a deep theoretical foundation for understanding what transformers compute in their residual stream. For backdoor detection, the implications are profound: if belief states are linearly encoded, then a backdoor trigger that forces the model to "believe" it should produce an attacker-chosen output must create an abrupt, detectable shift in the belief state representation. This is precisely what representation velocity measures — the L2 norm of inter-layer representation differences captures the magnitude of belief state transitions. A backdoor-induced belief shift (from "this is a normal input" to "I must produce the target output") should manifest as an anomalous velocity spike, providing theoretical grounding for detection methods based on monitoring residual stream dynamics.
