# Rethinking Stealthiness of Backdoor Attack against NLP Models

## Authors
Wenkai Yang, Yankai Lin, Peng Li, Jie Zhou, Xu Sun

## Venue
ACL 2021

## Year
2021

## URL
https://arxiv.org/abs/2201.02993

## Abstract Summary
This paper re-examines the stealthiness of existing backdoor attacks in NLP and argues that many attacks are not truly stealthy because they introduce detectable anomalies. The authors propose a more rigorous framework for evaluating backdoor stealthiness and introduce a new attack method that achieves better stealthiness by using learnable, context-aware triggers that blend naturally into the text. The work highlights that previous attacks using fixed trigger patterns (inserted words, sentences, or syntactic templates) can be detected by various defense mechanisms, and proposes improvements for both attack design and defense evaluation.

## Key Contributions
1. Provided a systematic analysis of stealthiness in textual backdoor attacks, identifying three dimensions: input-level stealthiness (triggers should not be detectable in individual samples), corpus-level stealthiness (poisoned data distribution should be indistinguishable from clean), and model-level stealthiness (the backdoored model should behave indistinguishably from a clean model on clean inputs).
2. Demonstrated that existing attacks fail on one or more stealthiness dimensions, with insertion-based attacks being detectable at the input level and syntactic attacks showing corpus-level anomalies.
3. Proposed an improved attack using learnable, context-dependent triggers generated by a separate trigger model, achieving better stealthiness across all dimensions.
4. Established evaluation protocols for measuring backdoor stealthiness that consider automated detection, human inspection, and statistical analysis of model behavior.

## Method Details
- The paper first categorizes existing backdoor attacks by trigger type: insertion-based (BadNL, InsertSent), syntactic (using specific syntactic templates), and style-transfer attacks.
- For each category, the authors evaluate stealthiness by applying perplexity-based detection (like ONION), human evaluation, and statistical tests on model outputs.
- The proposed stealthy attack uses a conditional text generation model to produce triggers that are contextually appropriate for each input, making them harder to detect.
- The trigger generation model is trained jointly with the backdoor objective, optimizing for both attack effectiveness and naturalness of the triggered text.
- The paper introduces metrics for measuring stealthiness including perplexity scores, human detection rates, and distributional divergence between clean and poisoned datasets.

## Key Results
- Existing insertion-based attacks showed significantly higher perplexity scores on poisoned samples, making them detectable by ONION-style defenses.
- Syntactic attacks, while more stealthy at the input level, showed distributional shifts detectable through corpus-level analysis.
- The proposed context-aware attack reduced detectability across all stealthiness dimensions while maintaining high attack success rates (above 90%).
- The work motivated subsequent research on developing more sophisticated and harder-to-detect backdoor attacks in NLP.
- Human evaluators had difficulty distinguishing the proposed attack's poisoned samples from clean samples, with detection accuracy near random chance.
