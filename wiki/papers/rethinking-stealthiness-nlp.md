# Rethinking Stealthiness of Backdoor Attack against NLP Models

## Summary

This paper re-examines the stealthiness of existing [[backdoor-attack]] methods in NLP and argues that many attacks introduce detectable anomalies. The authors propose a rigorous framework for evaluating stealthiness across three dimensions: input-level (triggers should not be detectable in individual samples), corpus-level (poisoned data distribution should match clean), and model-level (backdoored model should behave identically to clean on clean inputs).

The analysis reveals that insertion-based attacks are detectable at the input level by methods like [[onion]], while syntactic attacks show corpus-level anomalies. The paper proposes an improved attack using learnable, context-dependent triggers generated by a separate trigger model, achieving near-random-chance human detection rates.

## Method Details

The paper categorizes attacks by trigger type (insertion-based, syntactic, style-transfer) and evaluates each against three stealthiness dimensions using perplexity-based detection, human evaluation, and statistical tests.

The proposed stealthy attack uses a conditional text generation model to produce triggers that are contextually appropriate for each input. The trigger generation model is trained jointly with the backdoor objective, optimizing for both attack effectiveness and naturalness. Metrics include perplexity scores, human detection rates, and distributional divergence between clean and poisoned datasets.

## Results & Findings

- Insertion-based attacks show significantly higher perplexity on poisoned samples, making them detectable by [[onion]]-style defenses.
- Syntactic attacks show distributional shifts detectable through corpus-level analysis.
- The proposed context-aware attack reduced detectability across all dimensions while maintaining above 90% [[attack-success-rate]].
- Human evaluators had near-random-chance accuracy distinguishing poisoned from clean samples.

## Relevance to LLM Backdoor Defense

Understanding stealthiness dimensions is critical for designing robust defenses for LLMs. This work shows that defenses must operate at multiple levels (input, corpus, model) to catch sophisticated attacks. The context-aware trigger approach foreshadows attacks on LLMs that use natural instruction variations as triggers.
